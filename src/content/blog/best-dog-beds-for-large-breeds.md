Big dogs deserve big comfort — but finding a bed that actually holds up under 60, 80, or 100+ pounds is a challenge. Most "large" dog beds flatten within weeks, leaving your dog essentially sleeping on the floor with a decorative cover.

We spent time researching the beds that genuinely support large and giant breeds long-term, focusing on foam density, construction quality, cover durability, and whether they actually maintain their shape after months of use. Here are the five worth your money.

## What Large Breed Dogs Actually Need in a Bed

Large breeds aren't just bigger — they have different physical needs. Their weight puts more stress on joints (especially hips, elbows, and shoulders), they generate more body heat, and they can destroy flimsy beds in record time.

The most important factor is **foam density**. A bed might say "memory foam" on the label, but if the density is low (under 3 lbs/ft³), it'll compress flat quickly under a heavy dog. Look for beds with high-density support foam (4+ lbs/ft³) as a base layer, with a comfort layer of memory foam on top.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How thick should a dog bed be for a large breed?

For dogs over 60 lbs, we recommend at least 4 inches of foam, with 6-7 inches being ideal for dogs with joint issues. The thickness matters because a heavy dog will compress the foam more — a bed that feels plush to a 30-lb dog might bottom out under a 90-lb one. Always check the foam density (measured in lbs/ft³) in addition to thickness.

### Are orthopedic dog beds worth it for young, healthy dogs?

Yes. Joint issues in large breeds often develop gradually, and preventive support is easier than treatment. Starting your large breed puppy or young adult on a supportive bed can help protect their joints as they grow. Think of it like a good mattress for yourself — you don't wait until you have back problems to invest in quality sleep.

### How often should I replace a large dog bed?

It depends on the quality. Budget beds may need replacing every 6-12 months for heavy dogs. Mid-range beds typically last 1-2 years. Premium options like Big Barker can last 10+ years. A good test: press your hand into the bed where your dog usually lies. If you can feel the floor, it's time for a replacement.

### Should I get a raised/elevated bed instead?

Elevated cot-style beds (like Coolaroo) are great for warm climates and outdoor use, but they don't provide the orthopedic support that foam beds do. For joint health, a foam bed is better. Some owners use both — an elevated bed for warm days and a foam bed for sleeping.
